## Design Context

### Users

German teachers (Lehrkräfte) — professionals in their 30s–50s, often working in the evening or early morning, managing multiple classes, subjects, and administrative demands simultaneously. Their context when using Chalkdust is focused and time-pressured: they sit down to prepare and they need to feel like the tool is working with them, not against them. The job to be done: turn the accumulated knowledge of their classes into better, faster lesson plans — and build a record of what actually happened over time.

### Brand Personality

**Calm. Precise. Trustworthy.**

Chalkdust is the co-pilot that never panics. It handles complexity quietly and returns something clear. It doesn't shout. It doesn't dance. It thinks with you and gives you back control.

Emotional goal: when a teacher opens Chalkdust, they should feel *calm and in control* — like sitting down at a beautifully organised desk, knowing exactly where everything is.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Warm paper / analog** — cream and off-white tones, ink-like typography, the physical quality of a well-designed print piece. Think the texture of a teacher's desk: a good notebook, a quality fountain pen, a well-worn copy of a Lehrplan booklet with notes in the margins.

**Light-only** — no dark mode for now. The warmth of the palette *is* the identity. Forcing dark mode would strip it.

**Anti-references** — what this must never look like:
- Generic SaaS blue (the current design — bland, interchangeable)
- Schoolish or childish (crayons, blackboards, ABC motifs — condescending to adult professionals)
- Corporate bland (Notion-grey, everything-the-same, no visual identity)
- Startup loud (screaming CTAs, purple-to-pink gradients, "10x" energy)
- Toy-like or game-like (feels unprofessional for real educators)

**Color direction — to be surprised by.** The palette should feel unexpected but coherent. Consider:
- Warm parchment / cream background (`#F5F0E8` or similar)
- Deep ink black for type (`#1A1612`)
- A single vivid accent that earns its presence — not blue; consider warm amber, terracotta, forest moss, or a cool sage that contrasts with the warmth
- Muted secondary tones derived from the parchment base

**Typography** — strong, editorial, confident. Type should do the heavy lifting. Avoid the generic Inter/Geist stack for headings — consider a serif or a geometric display typeface for headlines. Body text can stay clean and sans-serif. Hierarchy through weight and size, not decoration.

**Composition** — don't play it safe. Asymmetry is welcome. Wide white (cream) margins. Let elements breathe. One strong focal point per section, not a grid of equal-weight cards.

### Design Principles

1. **Earn every pixel.** If an element doesn't carry meaning or guide the eye, remove it. Chalkdust is precise — its UI should be too.

2. **The paper desk, not the screen.** Every surface, texture, and color choice should evoke the physical craft of teaching: good paper, good ink, careful thought. Not tech, not software, not widgets.

3. **Calm scales.** When a teacher is stressed, the UI should reduce their cognitive load, not add to it. Calm is not boring — it is confident. Things are where they should be. Nothing surprises badly.

4. **Typography leads.** The product communicates through words (German, precise, professional). Let type carry visual hierarchy. Don't decorate what good type already achieves.

5. **One accent, used sparingly.** A single vivid accent color that appears only where it genuinely matters — primary actions, key data, active states. Everywhere else: warm neutrals. This restraint makes the accent powerful.
